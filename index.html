Methodology

How the simulation works — model design, tournament rules, tiebreakers, third-place qualification, and data sources.

Overview

Tournament probabilities are generated via 10,000 Monte Carlo simulations of the full FIFA World Cup 2026. Each simulation samples independent match outcomes from a statistical model, applies the official group-stage tiebreaker rules, assigns the 8 best third-place finishers to their R32 slots, and carries results through R32 → R16 → QF → SF → Final. Aggregating across 10,000 runs gives each team a probability distribution over every stage of the tournament.

The simulation runs hourly using live match data from football-data.org. As group matches complete, the model re-fits on observed results and group states are locked for finished matches — only the remaining matches in each group are simulated.

Prediction Model

Dixon-Coles Score Model

The primary match model is a Dixon-Coles bivariate Poisson model, which predicts the joint distribution of (home goals, away goals) for any match. It models each team's attacking and defensive strength as latent parameters, and includes a low-score correction factor (the Dixon-Coles ρ parameter) that adjusts for the empirically observed excess frequency of 0-0, 1-0, 0-1, and 1-1 scorelines.

The model is fitted on completed group-stage matches from WC 2026 using maximum likelihood estimation. Before sufficient live data is available, all teams' attacking and defensive parameters are initialised from a FIFA Elo prior (see below) so that the model produces sensible probabilities from match day 1.

FIFA-Ranking Elo Prior

Team strengths are seeded from FIFA rankings converted to an Elo-style rating using the official 2026 pre-tournament rankings. The Elo ratings are used to set the initial Dixon-Coles λ (attack) and μ (defence) parameters via a log-link, so that better-ranked teams start with higher expected goals for and lower expected goals against.

As live match data accumulates during the tournament, the Dixon-Coles MLE update increasingly dominates the Elo prior, which is down-weighted proportionally to the number of observed goals. This blended approach avoids both the cold-start problem (no data before MD1) and the over-reaction problem (treating a single group-stage result as highly informative about team quality).

Ensemble

Match win/draw/loss probabilities are drawn from the Dixon-Coles + Elo ensemble using a weighted combination of the two models' predictions. The weights are fixed at roughly 60% Dixon-Coles / 40% Elo at the start of the tournament, shifting toward 80/20 once each team has played 2+ games. All World Cup matches are treated as neutral-venue games.

Tournament Structure

Group Stage

WC 2026 has 48 teams across 12 groups (Groups A–L), each of 4 teams. Every team plays 3 group-stage matches (round-robin). Points: Win = 3, Draw = 1, Loss = 0.

Group Tiebreaker Rules

When teams are level on points, the official WC 2026 tiebreaker order is applied in sequence:

  1. Points in group
  2. Head-to-head (H2H) points among tied teams
  3. H2H goal difference among tied teams
  4. H2H goals scored among tied teams
  5. Overall goal difference
  6. Overall goals scored
  7. FIFA World Ranking (used as final deterministic tiebreaker in simulation)

H2H tiebreakers are computed on the sub-table of only the tied teams' mutual fixtures. If 3 or more teams are tied, the H2H rules are applied to the full sub-table of all tied teams simultaneously.

Third-Place Qualification

The 8 best third-place finishers across all 12 groups advance to the Round of 32. "Best" is determined by overall group-stage record (points → goal difference → goals scored) — H2H does not apply since third-place teams come from different groups. The 8 qualifiers are then assigned to specific R32 slots according to the official FIFA slot-assignment table, which maps which group the third-place team came from to which bracket position they occupy.

The 3rd-Place Guide tab shows the simulation's estimate of P(qualify | pts, GD), derived by tracking every third-place team across all 10,000 simulations and recording whether they made the cut.

Knockout Rounds

The bracket follows the official FIFA WC 2026 draw. R32 winners advance to R16, R16 winners to QF, QF winners to SF, and SF winners to the Final. No group-based seeding is applied in the knockout rounds — the bracket is fixed from the R32 draw. Extra time and penalty shootouts are simulated using the same match model for draws at full time.

Limitations

All probabilistic models of sporting outcomes have real limitations. These estimates are particularly sensitive to a few known issues:

  • Small sample size. Each team plays only 3 group-stage games, which is not enough data to precisely estimate team quality. The Elo prior partially mitigates this, but upsets and randomness remain high.
  • Squad injuries and rotations. The model has no knowledge of individual player availability. A key injury (e.g., a team's first-choice goalkeeper) is not reflected in the probabilities until it affects observable match outcomes.
  • Tournament fatigue and momentum. The model treats each match as an independent draw from the same team-strength distribution. Psychological momentum and fatigue — which empirically affect knockout performance — are not modelled.
  • Venue and altitude effects. All matches are treated as neutral-venue games. Host-nation USA benefits from crowd support; high-altitude venues in some group matches are not modelled.
